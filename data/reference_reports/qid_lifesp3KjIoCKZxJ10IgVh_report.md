# Bilateral Small-Finger Stiffness and Grip Weakness in Young Recreational Tennis Players: Most Likely Causes, Diagnostic Pathway, Evidence-Based Management, and Prevention

## Executive Summary
- In young tennis players with months of near-daily play and new bilateral small-finger stiffness, loss of active pinky flexion, and grip weakness, the leading causes are:
  - Ulnar neuropathy (cubital tunnel > Guyon’s canal), producing intrinsic muscle weakness and perceived stiffness and clumsiness in ulnar digits [1,14,15,27].
  - Flexor tendon sheath overuse disorders (stenosing tenosynovitis of the A1 pulley—trigger finger—and flexor tendinopathy), sometimes bilateral with repetitive gripping; morning stiffness and painful catching are typical [3,4,24].
  - Less frequent but relevant: annular pulley pathology (A2/A4) from gripping; sagittal band/extensor hood instability; early Dupuytren’s disease; inflammatory synovitis; and occult hook-of-hamate stress injury with deep branch ulnar neuropathy [5,6,7,8,10,11,12,38].
- First-line work-up: targeted history (stroke patterns, grip, handle setup, hypothenar pain; nocturnal elbow flexion), focused neuro-tendon exam, and point-of-care ultrasound (nerve cross-sectional area, A1 pulley thickness, pulley bowstringing, dynamic extensor hood) with selective electrodiagnostics (NCS/EMG) to localize neuropathy. Ultrasound has strong accuracy for ulnar neuropathy (medial epicondyle CSA threshold >10–10.5 mm²; AUC ~0.93), trigger finger (A1 thickness ~>0.62 mm), and pulley tears; MRI/CT are reserved for equivocal tendon/pulley findings or suspected hamate pathology [4,5,6,11,14,27,38].
- Red flags needing urgent referral: flexor tendon avulsion (absent active DIP flexion), rapidly progressive neurologic deficit/atrophy, pyogenic flexor tenosynovitis, severe vascular compromise, or suspected compartment syndrome [13,16,39,40].
- Management is condition-specific:
  - Ulnar neuropathy: activity modification, avoid prolonged elbow flexion and wrist extension/ulnar deviation, nocturnal elbow extension splinting, and hand therapy while confirming localization with EMG±ultrasound; surgery is reserved for progressive motor deficit, atrophy, or refractory cases [1,14,15,27].
  - Trigger finger: short trial of activity modification and splinting; corticosteroid injection into the A1 pulley is highly effective for symptom resolution in most patients; US guidance helps accurate placement; persistent or recurrent cases undergo A1 pulley release (open or percutaneous) [3,4,24].
  - Pulley injury: graded rest, taping/orthoses and progressive loading for low-grade lesions; surgical reconstruction only for high-grade tears with bowstringing or failure of conservative care [5,6,32].
  - Sagittal band: relative motion/yoke orthosis and activity modification for acute/subacute instability; surgery for chronic or severe instability [7,8].
  - Flexor tendon partial tears: imaging confirmation; nonoperative management for small/low-grade tears; surgical exploration/repair when high-grade or mechanical triggering persists [30,31].
- Prevention in tennis: progressive workload (avoid rapid volume spikes), coaching for less wrist-dominant strokes that reduce ulnar-sided load, appropriate grip size/handle cushioning and rational string tension, protective taping during load spikes, targeted hand/forearm/proximal-chain strengthening and warm-up, and self-monitoring (morning stiffness, fingertip control, grip dynamometry) [18,19,20].

## 1) Differential Diagnosis Focused on Racquet-Sport Overuse and Key Distinguishing Features

### A. Ulnar neuropathy at the elbow (cubital tunnel)
- Clinical pattern: paresthesia in ring/small fingers, intrinsic hand weakness (interossei/ADM), Wartenberg sign (abducted pinky), Froment sign (ulnar pinch weakness), worse with prolonged elbow flexion or night symptoms; may be bilateral with overuse [14,24].
- Diagnostics:
  - Ultrasound: ulnar nerve cross-sectional area (CSA) at the medial epicondyle >10–10.5 mm² provides the best sensitivity (~80.4%); nerve ratio metrics increase specificity; pooled AUC ~0.931 [14,27].
  - Electrodiagnostics: NCS/EMG localizes lesion and grades severity; recommended in conjunction with ultrasound by AANEM (Level B) [1].
  - Provocative tests alone have limited accuracy (e.g., elbow flexion-compression and Tinel’s at elbow show modest sensitivity/specificity) [25].

### B. Ulnar neuropathy at the wrist (Guyon’s canal)
- Clinical pattern: motor-predominant weakness of deep branch (FDI/ADM) with relative dorsal ulnar hand sensory sparing; symptoms provoked by hypothenar pressure and wrist extension/ulnar deviation on the racquet handle [15,28].
- Diagnostics: short-segment wrist conduction studies (recording from FDI) improve localization; ultrasound shows space-occupying lesions or anomalous muscles; consider hook-of-hamate pathology [2,15,28].

### C. Stenosing flexor tenosynovitis (A1 “trigger finger”) of the small finger
- Clinical pattern: palmar MCP tenderness/nodule, painful catching/locking, morning stiffness; can be bilateral/multi-digit with repetitive gripping [3,24].
- Ultrasound: A1 pulley thickening; a cut-off ~0.62 mm differentiates diseased from normal with good accuracy and correlates with intraoperative measures [4].

### D. Flexor tendinopathy or partial tears (FDP/FDS)
- Clinical pattern: longitudinal volar pain; pain/weakness on isolated FDP (DIP flexion) or FDS (PIP flexion) tests; complete FDP avulsion (jersey finger) usually acute and unilateral with loss of active DIP flexion [13,22,23,30,31].
- Imaging: ultrasound/MRI detect partial tears; in a cadaveric Zone II model, ultrasound had higher sensitivity than MRI for high-grade partial flexor tears (US ~0.50 vs MRI ~0.20; both had high specificity) [30].

### E. Annular pulley injuries (A2/A4 > A3)
- Clinical pattern: focal pulley pain; painful resisted flexion (especially with PIP extended); crepitus; bowstringing with higher-grade injuries; often from forceful gripping/pulling [5,6].
- Ultrasound: dynamic assessment with tendon-to-bone distance thresholds (~1.9–2.0 mm for A2/A4) demonstrates high diagnostic accuracy (Sn/Sp ~0.94–1.00) [5,6,32].

### F. Sagittal band/extensor hood injury at MCP (small finger)
- Clinical pattern: dorsal MCP pain/swelling; snapping or extensor tendon subluxation; difficulty initiating MCP extension; often traumatic but can be repetitive-use; instability can be demonstrated dynamically [7,8].

### G. Intrinsic tightness / lumbrical-plus
- Intrinsic tightness: positive Bunnell-Littler test (PIP flexion improves with MCP flexion) [21].
- Lumbrical-plus: paradoxical IP extension during attempted fist; classically after FDP injury/surgery; uncommon in pure overuse [9].

### H. Early Dupuytren’s disease
- Pattern: painless palmar nodules/cords (ring/small fingers), progressive extension contracture and a positive tabletop test; more common with increasing age; may be bilateral [10].

### I. Joint disease (synovitis/inflammatory arthritis)
- Pattern: small-joint swelling and prolonged morning stiffness; RA/PsA features (e.g., dactylitis, nail changes); ultrasound power Doppler supports synovitis; 2010 ACR/EULAR classification criteria apply to early RA [11,33,34].

### J. Hook of hamate stress fracture/nonunion with deep motor ulnar neuropathy
- Pattern: hypothenar tenderness, ulnar-sided grip weakness, deep motor branch deficits; radiographs can miss it; CT is reference standard; classically associated with racquet/bat sports [12,38].

### K. Critical rule-outs
- Flexor tendon avulsion/rupture (absent active DIP flexion): urgent hand surgery [13].
- Pyogenic flexor tenosynovitis: Kanavel’s signs—fusiform swelling, finger held in flexion, tenderness along sheath, pain with passive extension—require emergent care [16].
- Complex regional pain syndrome (clinical Budapest criteria) if disproportionate pain and autonomic changes [41,42].

## 2) Diagnostic Work-up, Accuracy, and Red Flags

### A. History: tennis-specific probes
- Dominant hand; hours/week and recent volume spikes; stroke patterns (e.g., extreme western forehand, two-handed backhand) that increase ulnar-sided load; handle grip size and cushioning; string tension changes; hypothenar impacts or pain; nocturnal elbow flexion; numbness/tingling; morning finger “locking”; dorsal MCP snapping; systemic inflammatory symptoms [18,19,20].

### B. Physical examination (key maneuvers)
- Ulnar neuropathy screen: sensory mapping (volar ring/small vs dorsal ulnar hand); intrinsic strength (finger ab/adduction); Froment, Jeanne, Wartenberg signs; Tinel at cubital tunnel/Guyon’s; elbow flexion-compression (recognizing limitations of provocative tests) [15,24,25].
- Flexor tendon isolation: FDP (DIP flexion with PIP stabilized), FDS (PIP flexion with adjacent fingers held in extension) to localize pain/weakness; note anatomic variants (absent small-finger FDS in some) [22,23].
- Trigger finger: palpate A1 pulley; observe catching/locking during active motion [24].
- Pulley assessment: palpate A2/A4 under resisted flexion with PIP extended; compare for bowstringing/crepitus [5,6].
- Sagittal band: with MCP flexed 30–45°, observe extensor tendon tracking during extension for snapping/subluxation [7,8].
- Intrinsic tightness: Bunnell-Littler test [21].
- Dupuytren’s: palmar nodules/cords, tabletop test [10].
- Infection red flags: Kanavel signs [16].
- Vascular screen if indicated; recognize limitations of the modified Allen test and consider Doppler if abnormal/equivocal [26].

### C. Imaging and electrodiagnostics
- Ultrasound:
  - Ulnar neuropathy at elbow: CSA thresholds at medial epicondyle (>10–10.5 mm²) provide good sensitivity (~80.4%); US is an accurate adjunct to EMG, especially in early disease [1,14,27].
  - Ulnar neuropathy at wrist: complements EMG in localizing Guyon’s canal lesions and detecting space-occupying causes [15,28].
  - Trigger finger: A1 pulley thickness >~0.62 mm distinguishes diseased from normal; dynamic US shows catching/hypervascularity [4].
  - Pulley injuries: dynamic bowstringing and tendon-to-bone distance cut-offs (~1.9–2.0 mm) for A2/A4 provide Sn/Sp ~0.94–1.00 across studies; A3 remains challenging [5,6,32].
  - Synovitis: US correlates with MRI for MCP/PIP synovitis (AUC ~0.91); power Doppler increases specificity [11].
- MRI:
  - Clarify equivocal tendon/pulley or extensor hood injuries; evaluate synovitis/tenosynovitis; assess suspected hamate pathology when radiographs are negative [7,8,29].
- Radiographs/CT:
  - Screen for avulsions, OA, calcifications (CPPD); hamate hook fractures are commonly missed on standard radiographs; CT is reference (high accuracy) and MRI shows marrow edema/associated nerve/tendon changes [38].
- Electrodiagnostics (NCS/EMG):
  - Indicated for suspected ulnar neuropathy, atypical sensory patterns, or to localize wrist vs elbow; short-segment wrist studies (FDI recording) improve detection/localization in Guyon’s canal [1,2,15].

### D. Practical algorithm for this presentation
1) Screen bilaterally for ulnar neuropathy (sensory map, intrinsic strength, Froment/Jeanne/Wartenberg, Tinel, elbow flexion-compression) and for tendon/pulley pathology (A1 tenderness/triggering; isolated FDP/FDS strength; pulley pain/bowstringing; dorsal MCP snapping). Document hypothenar tenderness [15,22,23,24,25].
2) Point-of-care ultrasound:
   - Ulnar nerve at elbow (CSA ± dynamic) and Guyon’s canal survey if motor-predominant pattern [14,15,27].
   - A1 pulley thickness/dynamic catching; A2/A4 tendon-to-bone distance/bowstringing; dynamic sagittal band [4,5,6,7].
3) If neuropathy suspected/persists: NCS/EMG with segmental elbow and wrist studies; correlate with US [1,2,15].
4) If hypothenar pain/ulnar neuropathy pattern: obtain wrist imaging; proceed to CT if hamate hook suspected [12,38].
5) If inflammatory arthropathy suspected: labs (ESR/CRP, RF, anti-CCP) and US of MCP/PIP with power Doppler; apply 2010 ACR/EULAR criteria for RA classification [11,33,34].

### E. Red flags needing urgent referral
- Absent active DIP flexion (suspect FDP avulsion/rupture) [13].
- Rapidly progressive intrinsic weakness/atrophy, severe pain out of proportion with autonomic changes (suspect CRPS or compartment syndrome), or vascular compromise [39,40,41,42].
- Kanavel signs consistent with pyogenic flexor tenosynovitis or septic arthritis [16].

## 3) Condition-Specific Management (2015–2025 Evidence Emphasis)

Note: Management should be individualized to diagnosis, severity, and athlete goals. Early, sport-specific hand therapy and graded return are preferred where feasible.

### A. Ulnar neuropathy (cubital tunnel and/or Guyon’s canal)
- Conservative care:
  - Activity modification: avoid prolonged elbow flexion (sleep/phone), valgus stress, and wrist positions that load the hypothenar region; adjust racquet grip and handle to reduce ulnar-side pressure [15,18,20,28].
  - Nocturnal elbow extension splinting and postural education are commonly used first-line while confirming localization by EMG/US [1,14,15].
  - Hand therapy: intrinsic strengthening and nerve protection strategies tailored to the location of entrapment [15].
- Procedure/surgery:
  - Indications: progressive motor deficit/atrophy, persistent symptoms with electrodiagnostic confirmation despite adequate conservative care, or structural compressive lesions (e.g., ganglion, hamate nonunion) [1,15,28,38].
  - Deep motor branch entrapment from hamate pathology often requires addressing the bony lesion (e.g., excision of the fractured hook in nonunion) [12,38].
- Diagnostic adjuncts:
  - AANEM recommends neuromuscular ultrasound as an adjunct to electrodiagnostics for UNE localization and confirmation (Level B) [1].

Comparative/accuracy notes:
- US for UNE at the elbow shows pooled AUC ~0.931; medial epicondyle CSA >10–10.5 mm² yields sensitivity ~80.4% with higher specificity using ratio metrics [14,27].
- Provocative tests at the elbow (Tinel, flexion-compression) have limited standalone diagnostic value [25].

### B. Stenosing flexor tenosynovitis (A1 “trigger finger”)
- At-home/splinting:
  - Short course of activity modification and MCP-block or ring splinting to reduce tendon-sheath friction can relieve symptoms in milder cases [3,24].
- Injection:
  - Corticosteroid injection into the flexor tendon sheath at the A1 pulley is effective for most symptomatic cases; ultrasound guidance improves accuracy of in-sheath placement and can visualize the A1 pulley and tendon [3,4,24].
- Surgery:
  - Persistent or recurrent trigger finger is treated with A1 pulley release (open or percutaneous), which has high success and low complication rates in appropriately selected patients [24].
- Imaging:
  - US A1 pulley thickness >~0.62 mm supports diagnosis and correlates with intraoperative thickening [4].

### C. Annular pulley injuries (A2/A4)
- Conservative:
  - Load reduction, taping/orthoses that reduce bowstringing, and progressive strengthening are first-line for partial/low-grade injuries; dynamic ultrasound helps grade and track recovery [5,6,32].
- Surgery:
  - Indicated for high-grade tears with significant bowstringing or failed conservative care; reconstruction options vary by grade and sport demands [5].
- Diagnostic accuracy:
  - Dynamic US with tendon-to-bone thresholds ≈1.9–2.0 mm for A2/A4 demonstrates high Sn/Sp (~0.94–1.00) compared with surgical/MRI reference standards [5,6,32].

### D. Sagittal band/extensor hood instability
- Conservative:
  - Relative motion (yoke) orthosis keeping the injured extensor centrally aligned, with activity modification; dynamic US guides diagnosis and monitoring [7].
- Surgery:
  - Consider for chronic, severe, or recurrent instability or when conservative measures fail; MRI can delineate tears when US is equivocal [7,8].

### E. Flexor tendon partial tears/tendinopathy
- Conservative:
  - For low-grade partial tears and tendinopathy: rest from provoking loads, controlled range, progressive loading, and tendon-gliding under hand therapy supervision; monitor with US/MRI if persistent symptoms [30,31].
- Surgery:
  - High-grade partial tears (mechanical triggering, significant thickness involvement) or failure of nonoperative care warrant surgical exploration/repair; avoid intratendinous injections [30,31].
- Imaging accuracy:
  - In Zone II partial flexor tears (cadaveric model), ultrasound outperformed MRI in sensitivity for high-grade lesions while both had high specificity [30].

### F. Early Dupuytren’s disease
- Observation for early nodular disease without contracture; interventions (e.g., needle fasciotomy, collagenase injection, or fasciectomy) are reserved for progressive contractures limiting function; less likely in young adult tennis players but relevant for bilateral small/ring finger involvement [10].

### G. Inflammatory arthropathies
- If suspected: prompt rheumatology referral; ultrasound with power Doppler aids early synovitis detection; apply 2010 ACR/EULAR criteria to classify RA; disease-modifying therapy is guided by rheumatology [11,33,34].

### H. Hook of hamate pathology with deep motor ulnar neuropathy
- Imaging-driven diagnosis (CT reference standard; MRI for marrow edema and associated nerve/tendon involvement); management may include immobilization for acute fractures or surgical excision of the hook in symptomatic nonunion with neuropathy; racquet sports are classic mechanisms [12,38].

### I. Infection (pyogenic flexor tenosynovitis)
- Emergent surgical irrigation and IV antibiotics; rely on Kanavel signs for diagnosis and urgency [16].

### J. Complex regional pain syndrome
- Clinical diagnosis using Budapest/Valencia criteria; early multidisciplinary management is recommended [41,42].

## 4) Return-to-Play (RTP) Considerations
- RTP depends on the specific diagnosis, symptom resolution, strength/control recovery, and sport demands:
  - Neuropathy: RTP is guided by resolution of provocative symptoms, restoration of intrinsic strength, and technique/equipment changes that reduce ulnar load; surgery requires longer rehabilitation when indicated [1,14,15,27,28].
  - Trigger finger: after successful injection or A1 release, RTP follows pain-free full motion and grip, typically after wound healing and early hand therapy when surgery is performed [24].
  - Pulley and extensor hood injuries: RTP follows pain-free function without bowstringing or instability, often guided by dynamic US; orthoses/taping may be used during graded return [5,7].
  - Flexor partial tears: RTP requires pain-free composite flexion and normal isolated FDP/FDS strength without triggering; imaging may confirm healing in high-demand athletes [30,31].
- Objective criteria to support RTP decisions include pain-free full ROM, symmetric intrinsic and grip strength (e.g., dynamometry), absence of triggering/snapping, negative provocative nerve tests in sport-specific positions, and intact proprioception/ball control on court drills [15,22,23,24].

## 5) Prevention Strategies Tailored to Tennis

### A. Workload and scheduling
- Avoid rapid spikes in weekly hitting volume and hard-court sessions; build volume progressively with rest days to limit tendon sheath thickening and nerve irritability [18,19].

### B. Technique and biomechanics
- Reduce wrist-dominant strokes and extreme ulnar deviation/extension during forehand/topspin phases that elevate ulnar-sided loads and hypothenar pressure; coaching to optimize kinematics across the kinetic chain can reduce hand/wrist overload [18,19].

### C. Equipment setup
- Use appropriate grip size (avoid undersized grips) and consider overgrips/handle padding to decrease hypothenar pressure; rational string tension (avoid excessive tension) can reduce vibration and peak handle forces [20].
- Reassess hand position on the handle to avoid focal pressure over Guyon’s canal/hook of hamate [15,20,28].

### D. Protective support during high-load phases
- For prior pulley symptoms, consider pulley-support taping/orthoses during volume spikes; for extensor hood instability, a relative motion/yoke orthosis can offload during return to play [5,7].

### E. Targeted conditioning and warm-up
- Regular hand/finger tendon-gliding, forearm flexor/extensor and intrinsic muscle strengthening, and proximal (shoulder/trunk) conditioning to distribute loads away from the wrist/hand; include sport-specific warm-ups emphasizing progressive grip/load exposure before high-intensity play [18,19].

### F. Self-monitoring for early recurrence
- Track:
  - Morning small-finger stiffness duration and any catching at the A1 pulley (early trigger symptoms) [24].
  - Ulnar symptoms when sleeping with elbows flexed or during prolonged driving (neuropathy screen) [14,24].
  - Hypothenar tenderness with gripping (flag for hamate/Guyon’s canal) [12,15].
  - Simple weekly grip dynamometry and fingertip control tasks; any ≥10–15% drop from baseline or recurrent snapping/locking should prompt deloading and reassessment [22,23,24].

## 6) Key Evidence Summaries (2015–2025; applicability to young adult recreational tennis)

- Ulnar neuropathy, elbow: Meta-analysis of 19 studies (1,961 exams) found US CSA at the medial epicondyle >10–10.5 mm² yields sensitivity ~80.4%, with nerve-ratio metrics increasing specificity; pooled AUC ~0.931 [14,27]. AANEM guideline recommends US as adjunct (Level B) to EMG for diagnosis/localization [1]. Provocative elbow tests have limited accuracy [25].
- Ulnar neuropathy, wrist: Segmental wrist conduction studies and US localize entrapments in Guyon’s canal and detect space-occupying lesions [2,15,28].
- Trigger finger: Clinical diagnosis; US A1 pulley thickness cut-off around 0.62 mm differentiates diseased vs normal and correlates with intraoperative measures [4]. Standard care includes splinting, corticosteroid injection, and A1 pulley release when needed [3,24].
- Annular pulleys: Dynamic US with tendon-to-bone distance thresholds (~1.9–2.0 mm for A2/A4) shows high Sn/Sp (~0.94–1.00) vs surgical/MRI reference; A3 injuries are harder to detect [5,6,32].
- Sagittal band: Dynamic US is well-suited to demonstrate tendon subluxation during motion; MRI can depict tears when dynamic tests are equivocal [7,8].
- Flexor partial tears: Cadaveric Zone II model shows ultrasound sensitivity higher than MRI for high-grade partial flexor tears (specificities high for both) [30]; clinical overviews guide thresholds for nonoperative vs surgical management [31].
- Synovitis: US correlates with MRI for small-joint synovitis (MCP/PIP AUC ~0.91); power Doppler improves specificity—useful when inflammatory arthritis is in the differential [11].
- Hook of hamate: CT is reference standard; standard radiographs miss a substantial proportion; racquet sports are classic mechanism; delayed diagnosis risks ulnar neuropathy/tendon injury [12,38].
- Infection and CRPS: Use Kanavel signs for flexor tenosynovitis and Budapest/Valencia criteria for CRPS; both require prompt, appropriate escalation [16,41,42].

## 7) Practical Plan for the Presented Scenario

- Most likely diagnoses: ulnar neuropathy (elbow > wrist), trigger finger of the small finger, and flexor/pulley overuse syndromes given bilaterality and repetitive gripping [3,4,5,14,15,18,20].
- In-clinic today: bilateral neuro-tendon exam; dynamic ultrasound of ulnar nerve (elbow ± wrist), A1 pulley, A2/A4 pulleys, and sagittal band; screen for hypothenar tenderness [4,5,6,7,14,15].
- Short-term management while work-up proceeds:
  - Deload tennis to tolerance; avoid prolonged elbow flexion and wrist extension/ulnar deviation on the handle; nocturnal elbow extension splints if neuropathy suspected [1,14,15].
  - MCP-block or ring splinting and activity modification if A1 tenderness/triggering is present; consider corticosteroid injection if symptoms persist or are function-limiting [3,24].
- Next tests:
  - NCS/EMG (elbow and wrist segments) if neuropathy signs/symptoms persist or for pre-intervention baseline and localization [1,2,15].
  - CT or MRI of wrist if hypothenar pain/ulnar motor pattern suggests hook-of-hamate pathology despite negative radiographs [12,38].
- Escalation:
  - Refer urgently for suspected tendon rupture or infection; consider surgical consultation for progressive intrinsic weakness or confirmed compressive lesion (e.g., hamate nonunion) [12,13,16,38].
