# Global Enterprise AI Services Market, 2024–2025

## Scope and definitions

Enterprise AI services in this brief encompass software and cloud-delivered capabilities used by organizations to build, deploy, govern, and consume AI in production. This includes, but is not limited to:
- Foundation-model access and managed inference (LLM APIs), model hosting, fine‑tuning/customization, vector databases, orchestration/agents, guardrails/safety, monitoring/evaluation, governance/compliance tooling, and MLOps; as well as packaged enterprise copilots integrated into productivity, workflow, and industry applications. This framing aligns with risk/governance and lifecycle concepts in the NIST AI Risk Management Framework (govern, map, measure, manage) and associated playbook, which many buyers now use to scope requirements and controls [14][15].

Assumptions and boundaries used here:
- Excludes raw compute/semiconductors and general IaaS spending unless bundled within a managed AI service; includes SaaS features where AI is a discrete SKU or material driver of ACV (e.g., enterprise “copilot” add‑ons).
- Where market‑sizing sources aggregate “AI” or “GenAI” across hardware, software, services, and industry solutions, those totals are used only as context; service‑only breakouts are noted where available.

## 1) Market size and growth (2024–2025)

What can be stated from the provided sources:
- AI and GenAI aggregate spend (software, services, and infrastructure) is growing quickly, led by the U.S. IDC projects U.S. AI spend reaching $336B by 2028, with financial services, software/information services, and retail as leading industries [21][61]. Gartner notes AI is a key driver of a 42% surge in data‑center systems spend in 2025, reflecting cost curves and capacity build‑outs that underpin AI services scaling [19][20].

Limitations and sensitivity:
- No directly comparable, source‑backed 2024/2025 revenue totals specifically for “enterprise AI services” were present in the provided research. Third‑party estimates vary widely based on scope choices (e.g., whether to include application layer copilots embedded in office/workflow suites; whether to include pro services tied to AI deployments; whether to net out infra).
- Given these scope ambiguities, a defensible approach is to triangulate from: (a) hyperscaler/ISV disclosures about AI feature ACV and usage; (b) industry vertical spend shares; and (c) macro AI spend growth signals. In the current evidence set, (a) manifests via ServiceNow’s AI‑attributed pipeline and revenue commentary [9][11], Google’s 85,000+ enterprises building with Gemini [22], and Microsoft Copilot seat expansions across large enterprises [6][62]; (b) and (c) via IDC and Gartner [21][61][19].

Recommendation to close the gap (primary sources for quantification):
- IDC Worldwide Artificial Intelligence and Generative AI Spending Guides (industry/technology splits) [21][61].
- Vendor segment disclosures in 10‑Ks/earnings for AI feature ARR/ACV (e.g., Microsoft, Google, ServiceNow) [6][22][11][62][9].

## 2) Adoption drivers in 2024–2025 (ranked, with evidence)

- Cost and productivity (rank #1)
  - 71% of respondents regularly use gen AI in at least one function; growing shares report revenue increases and meaningful cost reductions in deploying units (McKinsey State of AI) [1].
  - 67% of enterprises increased gen‑AI investment in Q3‑2024; 54% targeted efficiency/productivity, though only 38% tracked productivity gains (Deloitte) [3][2].
  - 83% of executives expect gen‑AI investments to rise; top goals include productivity and revenue growth; 78% expect ROI within 1–3 years (KPMG) [4].

- Developer velocity and software engineering acceleration (rank #2)
  - GitHub Copilot passed 20M all‑time users and is used by 90% of the Fortune 100 (July 2025) [5].
  - Microsoft reported its largest quarter of Copilot seat adds since launch, with large‑scale expansions (e.g., Barclays to 100k seats; UBS enterprise‑wide) [6][62].

- Integration with enterprise platforms and workflows (rank #3)
  - ServiceNow reported “Now Assist” (gen‑AI) as its fastest‑growing new product in 2024, with multiple $1M+ deals and doubled net‑new ACV QoQ, and reiterated AI as a core growth driver in 2025 [9][11].

- Security, governance, and compliance (rank #4)
  - U.S. federal OMB policy M‑24‑10 requires safeguards, governance structures, and transparency for AI affecting rights/safety—setting concrete buyer requirements for evaluations and monitoring [12][13].
  - NIST AI RMF 1.0 and its Playbook are widely used baselines for AI risk controls in procurement and internal governance [14][15].
  - The EU AI Act entered into force July 12, 2024, with obligations phasing in through 2025–2026, pushing enterprises and vendors toward risk‑based conformity, transparency, and governance [16][34][17][35].

- Quality/accuracy and trust (rank #5)
  - The UK ICO is actively consulting on how the data‑protection “accuracy” principle applies to gen‑AI outputs—raising the bar for evaluations, explainability, and accuracy controls in enterprise deployments [18].

- Total cost of ownership and scaling economics (rank #6)
  - Gartner highlights AI’s role in accelerating data‑center spend and notes many 2024 pilots did not proceed, emphasizing ROI discipline and TCO scrutiny [19][20].

## 3) Leading customer segments

- Financial services
  - IDC indicates banking/financial services will account for over 20% of global AI spend through 2028, making it the leading industry by AI outlays [21][61].
  - Earnings commentary repeatedly cites large banks scaling AI assistants and productivity tools (e.g., Microsoft Copilot expansions) [6][62].

- Technology/software and information services
  - Alphabet reports “more than 85,000 enterprises” building with Gemini on Google Cloud, with 35× YoY usage growth, indicating strong developer‑led adoption in tech and adjacent sectors [22][23].
  - GitHub Copilot adoption (20M all‑time users; 90% of Fortune 100) underscores deep developer uptake [5].

- Healthcare
  - Nuance (Microsoft) DAX Copilot has been adopted by more than 400 healthcare organizations, with reported clinician time savings and quality improvements [24][25].
  - FDA finalized guidance enabling Predetermined Change Control Plans (PCCP) for AI‑enabled device software, facilitating lifecycle updates within validated bounds and supporting scale in regulated clinical workflows [26][27].

- Government/public sector
  - U.S. federal policy sets strict governance obligations, catalyzing demand for enterprise‑grade controls in acquisitions [13].
  - Palantir reported Q2‑2025 U.S. government revenue +53% YoY and record $2.3B TCV in the quarter, signaling strong AI demand in defense and civilian agencies [28][29].
  - Gartner projects fewer than 25% of government organizations will have gen‑AI citizen‑facing services by 2027, reflecting a cautious external posture (focus on internal use first) [30].

Geographic skew
- The U.S. leads AI spend in absolute terms, with Western Europe and China following, per IDC’s regional view; the U.S. AI spend is projected at $336B by 2028 [21].

## 4) Regulatory and compliance barriers (by region and sector)

United States (cross‑sector)
- OMB policy M‑24‑10 mandates agency AI governance structures, impact assessments, independent evaluations, and transparency for AI affecting rights/safety; these expectations cascade to vendors via acquisition and compliance requirements [12][13].
- NIST AI RMF 1.0 and Playbook provide a common risk/governance baseline increasingly referenced by both public and private buyers [14][15].
- Executive Orders in January 2025 shifted the federal AI policy landscape while leaving OMB’s governance requirements in place unless separately rescinded [31][32].

United States (sector overlays)
- Financial services: SR 11‑7 (Federal Reserve/OCC) remains the bedrock for Model Risk Management, now applied to ML/AI including vendor‑supplied models (inventory, validation, monitoring, governance) [33].
- Healthcare: FDA’s PCCP guidance enables pre‑authorized lifecycle updates for AI‑enabled device software, contingent on robust change controls and post‑market monitoring [26][27].

European Union
- AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689) entered into force July 12, 2024. Obligations phase in across 2025–2026 for high‑risk AI (conformity assessment/CE marking, risk management) and general‑purpose AI model providers, with transparency and governance expectations shaping enterprise procurement [16][34][17][35].

United Kingdom
- ICO guidance emphasizes explainability, fairness, and accuracy for AI under UK data‑protection law; the ICO is actively executing an AI and biometrics plan of action [36][37][18].
- PRA SS1/23 formalizes model risk management principles for banks, covering AI/ML models and vendor use [38].

Japan
- APPI governs personal data; the government published “AI Guidelines for Business Ver. 1.0” (Apr 19, 2024) and launched a national AI Safety Institute for testing and evaluation [39][40][41][42]. Japan’s copyright exceptions have influenced developer views on training data, though debates continue [43].

Singapore
- PDPA sets data‑protection obligations; Singapore’s Model AI Governance Frameworks (original and 2024 Generative AI framework) provide practical governance guidance for enterprises and agencies [44][45][46].

Australia
- Privacy Act (APPs) governs personal information; the ASD’s IRAP program and cloud assessment guidance are frequently required for government workloads; the DTA sets AI use policies for agencies [47][48][49][50].

India
- Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 (DPDP) is the baseline privacy law; 2024 advisories to platforms address deepfakes/misinformation and labeling obligations for unreliable/under‑tested AI models [51][52][53].

China
- PIPL governs personal data with stringent cross‑border transfer requirements; CAC’s 2023 generative AI measures regulate public deployment; 2024 provisions relaxed and clarified cross‑border data transfers with thresholds/exemptions; 2025 guidance further facilitates cross‑border flows of financial data—materially impacting model training/serving footprints and data residency [54][55][56][57][58][59][60].

Practical buyer expectations across sectors (illustrative)
- Governance/risk: Alignment to NIST AI RMF (or local equivalents), documented model lineage/datasets, evaluations and monitoring plans [14][46].
- Data residency/flows: EU AI Act overlays on GDPR; China’s 2024–2025 cross‑border rules materially shape architecture decisions [34][57][60].
- Accuracy/quality: UK ICO focus on accuracy in gen‑AI outputs drives procurement checklists [18].
- Sector controls: SR 11‑7 and PRA SS1/23 for financial services; FDA PCCP and medical software guidances for healthcare [33][38][26][27].

## 7) Medium‑term outlook (2–5 years) with scenarios, risks, and catalysts

Signals and leading indicators
- Demand: High and rising enterprise intent to invest (McKinsey, Deloitte, KPMG) [1][3][4].
- Capacity: Data‑center systems spend surging due to AI (Gartner) [19][20].
- Regulation: EU AI Act obligations phasing in; U.S. OMB governance policy in force; China easing some cross‑border flows, especially for financial data [16][34][17][35][12][13][57][60].
- Enterprise proof points: Large rollouts of copilots in technology and financial services; healthcare ambient AI scaling; strong U.S. government AI platform purchases [6][62][24][25][28][29][22].

Scenario framework (2026–2029)

- Base case
  - Enterprises consolidate on a mix of frontier APIs and open/custom models, with governance, observability, and TCO optimization as primary selection criteria. Adoption deepens in software development, customer operations, knowledge work, and select industry workflows (e.g., healthcare documentation, financial ops).
  - Growth is paced by evaluation rigor (NIST AI RMF), EU AI Act/HQ compliance obligations, and data localization in China. U.S. remains the largest market; Western Europe ramps post‑AI Act conformity infrastructure; APAC growth is heterogeneous [14][16][34][57].
  - Indicators: Continued Copilot/Workspace AI seat expansions; platform AI ACV disclosures by ISVs (e.g., ServiceNow); steady U.S. government AI TCV [6][62][11][28].

- Bull case
  - Rapid cost curve improvements (hardware, inference efficiency) unlock broader agentic automation and domain‑tuned reasoning in production; credible evals and guardrails mitigate operational/brand risk. EU conformity processes streamline; China’s cross‑border rules continue to ease for regulated sectors [19][57][60].
  - Indicators: Step‑change in data‑center capacity deployments, vendor margin improvement on AI SKUs, wider publication of enterprise benchmark wins tied to specific tasks, acceleration of high‑risk system conformity in EU.

- Bear case
  - Regulatory friction (EU AI Act conformity bottlenecks; fragmented national rules), persistent model errors, content/IP litigation drag, and hardware/power constraints slow production scaling; ROI disappointments lead to program resets and narrower scoping [16][34][35][19].
  - Indicators: Rising program cancellations (echoing 2024 pilot attrition), slowing seat growth, higher TCO/latency from supply constraints, delayed conformity assessments in the EU, stricter government procurement bars.

Key risks
- Regulatory shifts and compliance costs (EU AI Act conformity; model transparency/traceability; sector MRM/FDA) [16][34][33][26].
- Open‑source and self‑hosted competition compressing API margins; data residency constraints increasing multi‑region OPEX (China PIPL/CBDT) [54][57].
- Hardware/power constraints inflating inference costs (Gartner data‑center spend signal) [19].
- Trust/safety incidents and IP/content licensing disputes affecting enterprise willingness to deploy at scale (UK ICO accuracy focus; sector regulators) [18][33].

Catalysts
- Demonstrable, audited productivity gains tied to specific workflows (e.g., developer velocity, contact center deflection) reflected in earnings/ARR disclosures [6][22][11].
- Regulatory clarity and operational playbooks (NIST AI RMF adoption; FDA PCCP utilization; EU AI Act secondary standards) [14][26][16].
- Vendor distribution through entrenched platforms (M365/GitHub, ServiceNow, Google Workspace/Cloud) with favorable unit economics [6][9][22].
